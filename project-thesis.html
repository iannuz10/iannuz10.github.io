Master's Thesis: Development and Evaluation of Supernumerary Robotic Limb Control Strategies and Sense of Embodiment in a Virtual Reality Environment

A comprehensive user study in a custom-built UE5 environment, investigating how wearable robotic limbs compare to external cobots and how users can feel 'in control' of an autonomous partner.

The Challenge (Situation & Task)

Supernumerary Robotic Limbs (SRLs) are a promising form of human augmentation, but their widespread adoption is hindered by challenges in designing intuitive control schemes and inducing a strong sense of embodiment in users. A significant bottleneck in the field is the lack of standardized, cost-effective, and adaptable platforms for systematic experimentation.

The primary goal of this thesis was to address this gap by developing and validating a modular, high-fidelity Virtual Reality environment to serve as a standardized testbed for SRL research. The objective was to create a platform capable of systematically comparing different robotic assistance paradigms and their effects on user performance, workload, and subjective experience.

My Solution (Action)

To tackle this challenge, I designed and built a complete VR application from the ground up using Unreal Engine 5. The development process involved several key actions:

  • VR Environment Creation: I developed a realistic and interactive virtual environment centered around a complex multi-step assembly task (building a stool). This included implementing a robust physics-based interaction system for object manipulation, part attachment, and functional tool usage (hammer, screwdriver, etc.).
  • System & Control Implementation: I implemented multiple SRL control strategies, with a primary focus on an autonomous assistant driven by a Finite State Machine (FSM) that could proactively help the user by handling parts and tools.
  • User Study Design & Execution: I conducted a formal within-subjects user study with 24 participants. The experiment compared performance and user experience across three distinct conditions: (1) working unassisted, (2) collaborating with the autonomous wearable SRL, and (3) collaborating with an autonomous external cobot.
  • Data Analysis: I collected and processed a rich dataset, including objective performance metrics (completion time, 3D hand path data, error counts) and subjective feedback from validated questionnaires (NASA-TLX, UEQ-S, VEQ). Custom Python scripts were written for detailed statistical analysis.

Results & Takeaways (Result)

The study yielded several significant findings that contribute to the field of human-robot interaction:

  • Performance Boost: Both the SRL and Cobot assistants led to significantly faster task completion times, reduced physical effort (shorter hand paths, smaller workspace), and lower perceived workload compared to working alone.
  • The Superiority of Wearable Systems: While functionally equivalent, users showed a clear subjective preference for the wearable SRL. It was perceived as significantly more "inventive," "lifelike," and "intelligent" than the external cobot, resulting in a more engaging user experience.
  • Embodiment of an Autonomous Agent: A key discovery was that users reported a strong sense of Ownership and, counterintuitively, Agency over the SRLs, despite having no direct manual control. This suggests that predictable, task-relevant autonomous behavior can generate a powerful feeling of control.
  • Embodiment as a Driver of Preference: Further analysis revealed that this sense of embodiment was a key predictor of user preference. Participants who felt a stronger connection to the SRL were significantly more likely to prefer it, highlighting that subjective feeling is as important as objective performance.
